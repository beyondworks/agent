## FULL NARRATION SCRIPT (English)

### HOOK (0:00~0:20)

On June 22nd, 1986, in front of 114,000 screaming fans at the Azteca Stadium, one man did the impossible. He grabbed the ball in his own half — and ran. Past one defender. Then another. Then another. Sixty yards, ten seconds, five defenders and a goalkeeper — all left behind. Four minutes earlier, that same man had cheated. He punched the ball into the net with his fist and got away with it.

His name was Diego Armando Maradona. And this is the story of how the greatest footballer who ever lived destroyed himself.

---

### ACT 1: THE BOY FROM THE SLUMS (0:20~2:30)

Villa Fiorito. A shantytown on the edge of Buenos Aires, where the streets had no names and the houses had no floors. This is where Diego was born on October 30th, 1960. His father worked in a factory. His mother raised eight children in a house smaller than most people's living rooms.

Diego got his first football at age three. And from the moment he touched it, everyone knew something was different about this boy. By eight years old, he was performing halftime tricks at professional matches. His youth team, Las Cebollitas — "The Little Onions" — won 136 consecutive games. One hundred and thirty-six. Without a single loss.

At fourteen, he signed with Argentinos Juniors. At fifteen, he was training with grown men twice his size. And on October 20th, 1976 — ten days before his sixteenth birthday — Diego Armando Maradona made his professional debut. He was the youngest player to ever set foot on an Argentine professional pitch.

But talent born in poverty comes with a price. The boy who had nothing suddenly had everything. And no one taught him how to carry it.

---

### ACT 2: THE RISE (2:30~4:30)

By twenty-one, Maradona had scored 115 goals for Argentinos Juniors. Boca Juniors bought him, and he won the Argentine league in his first season. Then the world came calling.

In 1982, FC Barcelona paid five million pounds — a world record transfer fee — for a twenty-one-year-old from the slums. But Barcelona was a disaster. Hepatitis struck first. Then, on September 24th, 1983, Athletic Bilbao defender Andoni Goikoetxea shattered his ankle with a tackle so vicious it nearly ended his career. Maradona lasted two seasons. Thirty-eight goals in fifty-eight games — incredible numbers for a broken man.

Then came Naples.

On July 5th, 1984, Diego Maradona arrived at Stadio San Paolo by helicopter. Seventy-five thousand Neapolitans were waiting inside. Not for a football match. Just for him. They chanted: "Ho visto Maradona" — I have seen Maradona.

Naples was the poorest city in Italy. The north mocked them. But Maradona gave them something they never had: pride. He won Napoli their first-ever Serie A title in 1987. Then another in 1990. A UEFA Cup. A Coppa Italia. He didn't just play for Naples — he became their god. Murals of his face still cover the walls of the city today. They call him D10S — a word that fuses "Dios," the Spanish word for God, with the number 10 on his back.

---

### ACT 3: THE PEAK — JUNE 22, 1986 (4:30~7:00)

The 1986 World Cup in Mexico. This is where Diego Maradona became immortal.

Argentina had been knocked out early in 1982. The country was reeling from the Falklands War with England. When the two nations met in the quarter-finals, it wasn't just football. It was war by other means.

The score was zero-zero at halftime. Then, in the fifty-first minute, the ball deflected into the air off England's Steve Hodge. Maradona and goalkeeper Peter Shilton both jumped for it. Shilton was taller. But Maradona's fist got there first.

He punched the ball into the net. The referee didn't see it. One-nil.

After the match, a reporter asked him how the goal was scored. Maradona smiled and said: "A little with the head of Maradona, and a little with the hand of God."

But what happened four minutes later made the world forget the cheating.

Fifty-fifth minute. Maradona receives the ball in his own half, back to goal. He turns. And he runs. Past Peter Beardsley. Past Peter Reid. He accelerates into the England half — Terry Fenwick lunges, Maradona shifts his weight and the defender grabs nothing but air. Terry Butcher closes in. Maradona goes around him. Butcher recovers, tries again. Maradona goes around him a second time. Now it's just him and Shilton. The goalkeeper spreads himself wide. Maradona drags the ball past him with his left foot and slides it into an empty net.

Sixty yards. Ten seconds. Five outfield players and a goalkeeper. The greatest goal ever scored.

In the final, Argentina faced West Germany. The score was 2-2 with six minutes to go. Maradona, double-marked the entire match, found one moment of space. He threaded a perfect pass to Jorge Burruchaga, who scored the winner. Argentina were world champions.

Maradona didn't just win the 1986 World Cup. He carried an entire nation on his back. Five goals, five assists — he was involved in ten of Argentina's fourteen goals. He attempted ninety dribbles, three times more than any other player. He was fouled fifty-three times. And he won the Golden Ball by unanimous vote.

It was the single greatest individual performance in the history of team sports.

---

### ACT 4: THE FALL (7:00~9:00)

The god had a demon. And the demon's name was cocaine.

In Naples, the parties never stopped. The Camorra — the Neapolitan mafia — surrounded him. The women. The drugs. The lifestyle that comes when an entire city worships you as a living deity.

On March 17th, 1991, a random drug test revealed cocaine in his system. Fifteen months banned. His time in Naples was over. The city that had worshipped him watched him leave in disgrace.

He went to Sevilla. Then back to Argentina. But the drugs never stopped.

In 1994, Maradona returned to the World Cup in the United States. He was thirty-three. Overweight. Out of shape. But on June 21st, against Greece, he scored a goal and ran to the sideline camera, screaming into the lens with bulging eyes. It was terrifying. It was electric. And it was the last great moment of his career.

Four days later, after Argentina beat Nigeria two to one, FIFA announced Maradona had tested positive for five variants of ephedrine. He was expelled from the tournament. His World Cup — and his career at the highest level — was over.

His own explanation: his trainer had bought the American version of an energy drink that contained a banned substance, unlike the Argentine version he was used to. No one believed him.

---

### ACT 5: THE END (9:00~10:30)

After retirement, the man who could do anything with a football could do nothing without one. His weight ballooned to 280 pounds. In 2000, he nearly died from a cocaine-induced heart attack. In 2004, another cardiac emergency. In 2005, he underwent gastric bypass surgery in Colombia.

He tried coaching. Argentina's national team from 2008 to 2010 — eliminated by Germany four-nil in the World Cup quarter-finals. Then clubs in Dubai, Mexico, back to Argentina. His final post was at Gimnasia y Esgrima La Plata. He was still their coach when it happened.

On November 25th, 2020, Diego Armando Maradona suffered cardiac arrest in his sleep. He was sixty years old.

At his funeral, held in the Casa Rosada — the presidential palace of Argentina — hundreds of thousands lined the streets of Buenos Aires, in the middle of a global pandemic, to say goodbye. His heart, doctors later revealed, weighed nearly twice the normal size. His liver and kidneys were severely damaged.

Eight members of his medical staff are currently standing trial for negligent homicide.

---

### CLOSING (10:30~10:50)

Diego Maradona was born with nothing in a shantytown with no name. He became the greatest footballer the world has ever seen. He was worshipped as a god. And he destroyed himself with the same hands that once touched the divine.

He was not a perfect man. But he played perfect football. And for those who saw him — truly saw him — nothing else ever came close.
